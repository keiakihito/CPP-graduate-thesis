\chapter{Methodology and Experimental Design}
\label{ch:methodology}

This chapter presents the experimental framework used to examine the influence of embedding-model capacity on similarity-based retrieval performance. 
The methodology follows a hypothesis-driven, iterative experimental design. 
A baseline retrieval pipeline is first established to ensure controlled and reproducible evaluation, after which experimental conditions are progressively expanded to evaluate the core research hypothesis under varying capacity regimes.


\section{Research Intent and Experimental Guarantees}
This section defines the core research property under investigation and clarifies the guarantees provided by the experimental design.

\subsection{Core Research Property}

The relationship between embedding-model capacity and retrieval performance is hypothesized to be condition-dependent. 
Specifically, in small-scale, single-domain archives, increased capacity does not necessarily guarantee monotonically superior retrieval effectiveness.

\subsection{Experimental Guarantees}
The experimental design provides the following guarantees:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Isolation of Scaling Effects:} 
    Parameter scaling effects are examined within each architectural family (CNN and Transformer) via small, base, and large variants.
    Cross-family comparisons (CNN vs.\ Transformer) are interpreted as evaluations of practical model complexity regimes, in which parameter count, architecture, and pretraining objectives co-vary.

    \item \textbf{Detection of Diminishing Returns:} 
    By comparing models across a logarithmic capacity scale, the design enables observation of diminishing returns within the tested capacity range.

    \item \textbf{Falsifiability:} 
    The hypothesis is falsifiable if higher-capacity models consistently outperform lower-capacity baselines across all proxy tasks without evidence of saturation.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Metadata and Label Taxonomy}
Ground truth for proxy retrieval tasks is derived from editorial metadata associated with each recording. The composer metadata serves as an objective label for sanity-check retrieval tasks, allowing us to verify if models can cluster tracks by composition. For the primary musical proxy task, tracks are annotated with abstract character labels (e.g., ``Heavy'' versus ``Light''), which capture the timbral and structural qualities relevant to the archive's curatorial goals.

\subsection{Audio Preprocessing Pipeline}

To ensure internal validity, the audio preprocessing pipeline is held constant across all embedding models.

Source recordings, originally distributed in compressed formats (e.g., MP3 from CD albums), are first converted to uncompressed WAV format to avoid variability introduced by compression artifacts. 
All audio files are resampled to [e.g., 16 kHz or 32 kHz] in accordance with the input requirements of the evaluated models. 
Channel configuration is standardized to [mono/stereo], and amplitude normalization (e.g., peak normalization) is applied to ensure consistent signal scaling across recordings.

In the primary experimental setup, each complete track serves as a single retrieval unit. 
Embeddings are therefore derived at the track level, treating each full recording as one input instance. 
For models requiring fixed-length inputs, representative segments are extracted using a consistent windowing strategy (e.g., uniform segmentation with aggregation).

Frame- or token-level representations produced by the model are aggregated using global mean pooling across the temporal dimension, resulting in a single fixed-dimensional embedding vector per track. 
This pooling strategy is applied consistently across all evaluated models to ensure that performance differences are attributable to embedding-model capacity rather than aggregation mechanisms.

Although classical works are often structured into multiple movements, movement-level segmentation is not applied in the primary experiments. 
Each complete track serves as the unit of retrieval analysis in order to maintain a consistent and controlled evaluation setting.

Movement-level metadata is retained for potential future analyses. 
However, segmentation-based experiments are considered beyond the scope of the present study. 
Such extensions may enable examination of whether embedding-model capacity interacts differently with finer-grained structural units.


\section{Selection of Embedding Models (Independent Variable)}

Models are selected to represent discrete capacity tiers within two major architectural families: CNN-based and Transformer-based models. 
Model capacity is operationalized primarily through parameter count ($|\theta|$). 
Within each family, three capacity levels (small, base, and large) are selected to enable controlled scaling comparisons under a shared architectural backbone.

This design serves two complementary purposes. 
First, comparisons within each architectural family (e.g., small vs.\ base vs.\ large) isolate parameter scaling effects while holding core architectural principles constant. 
Second, cross-family comparisons (CNN vs.\ Transformer) provide insight into how architectural inductive biases interact with model capacity under matched or comparable capacity regimes. 
This structured design ensures that capacity effects are analyzed both within and across architectural families without conflating scaling and structural differences.

\section{The Retrieval Pipeline}

\subsection{Inference Engine}
The retrieval system is implemented as a modular backend pipeline that isolates the embedding extraction process from downstream retrieval tasks. The system is built using the PyTorch and HuggingFace Transformers frameworks and executed on [TBD: Hardware Specs, e.g., NVIDIA A100 / RTX 3090] hardware to ensure efficient batch processing.

\subsection{Similarity and Indexing}
Although embedding dimensionality varies across models, cosine similarity is retained as a standardized similarity measure due to its scale invariance and widespread adoption in representation learning literature. 
While high-dimensional spaces may exhibit concentration effects, all models are evaluated under identical similarity and ranking procedures. Because performance is assessed through rank-based metrics rather than raw similarity magnitudes, cross-model comparisons remain meaningful even under dimensional differences. Observed trends are therefore interpreted as arising from representational quality rather than dimensional artifacts alone.
Future extensions may include dimensionality normalization or projection (e.g., PCA alignment) to further disentangle capacity effects from embedding dimensionality. However, such transformations are excluded from the present study to preserve model-native representational characteristics.



\section{Evaluation Protocol (Dependent Variables)}
Each track in the archive is used as a query in a leave-one-out retrieval setting, where the query item itself is excluded from the candidate pool.
Performance metrics are averaged across all query instances to obtain corpus-level estimates.

\subsection{Relevance Judgments (Ground Truth)}
Relevance is determined by metadata matching as defined in Chapter 3. The first proxy task, the Sanity Proxy, defines relevance based on shared composer identity; a retrieved item is considered relevant only if it shares the same composer as the query item. The second task, the Primary Musical Proxy, defines relevance based on abstract musical character labels; items are relevant if they share the same descriptive character (e.g., ``Heavy'') as the query.

\subsection{Performance Metrics}
Because relevance labels are binary and the dataset is relatively small, ranking ties may occur when multiple relevant items share identical similarity scores. To ensure deterministic evaluation, a consistent tie-breaking strategy (e.g., lexicographic ordering by track identifier) is applied across all models. While NDCG can operate under binary relevance, Precision@K is additionally reported to mitigate potential saturation effects in cases where a large proportion of the archive satisfies the relevance criterion. These precautions preserve the interpretability of ranking-based comparisons across capacity tiers.

Prior to full-scale experimentation, a preliminary validation was conducted to ensure the functional integrity of the retrieval pipeline. 
Embedding generation, similarity computation, and ranking procedures were verified to operate deterministically and without degeneration. 
As this validation pertains to implementation reliability rather than empirical findings, detailed results are omitted.



\section{Experimental Design}
The empirical evaluation proceeds in three structured stages.

\paragraph{Stage I: Composer-Based Sanity Retrieval}
This stage evaluates whether models can retrieve items sharing the same composer identity, establishing a functional baseline.

\paragraph{Stage II: Musical Character Retrieval}
This stage evaluates retrieval performance under abstract musical-character constraints, directly testing the core hypothesis under domain-specific similarity definitions.

\paragraph{Stage III: Capacity–Performance Analysis}
This stage analyzes the relationship between model capacity and retrieval metrics, examining whether performance trends exhibit monotonic growth, saturation, or degradation across capacity tiers.




\subsection{Failure Modes and Boundary Conditions}
These boundary scenarios function as structural tests of the central hypothesis rather than unexpected empirical outcomes.
If performance differences align strictly with architectural class rather than parameter count within the same family, this would suggest that inductive bias, rather than raw capacity, governs retrieval effectiveness under the tested domain conditions.
We explicitly define three failure scenarios and their implications for the hypothesis:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Strict Monotonic Improvement:} If retrieval performance improves linearly or log-linearly with model capacity across all proxies, the condition-dependence hypothesis is weakened, suggesting that capacity and modern large-scale pretraining strategies may act as dominant drivers under the tested domain conditions.
    \item \textbf{No Performance Variation:} If all models perform equivalently (within a statistical margin), it implies that the proxy tasks or the dataset are insufficient to discriminate between embedding qualities, representing a validity threat to the experimental design. Such an outcome may reflect metric saturation under binary relevance rather than true equivalence between models, and would therefore motivate refinement of the proxy definition.
    \item \textbf{Performance Degradation:} If higher-capacity models perform worse than compact baselines, it provides strong evidence for the hypothesis, suggesting overfitting or domain mismatch (e.g., pretraining bias) in the larger models.
\end{enumerate}
