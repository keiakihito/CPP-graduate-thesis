# RecSys Core Claim — System Design Framing

## Core claim
In a content-based music recommender system for a small-scale classical archive, higher-capacity pretrained audio embedding models do not consistently outperform medium-sized alternatives on candidate ranking quality, despite incurring substantially higher computational cost. System designers therefore cannot treat model scaling as a reliable lever for improving candidate generation quality in data-constrained, cold-start archival settings; the benefit of additional capacity is conditioned on task structure, and the cost is unambiguously real.

## Capacity behavior
Increasing embedding-model capacity produces non-monotonic and architecturally inconsistent scaling behavior. Within the CNN family, ranking quality (NDCG@5) dips from the small to the medium tier before recovering at the large tier. Within the Transformer family, the medium-capacity model outperforms its larger counterpart across all reported metrics. Neither family exhibits a uniformly increasing capacity–performance curve, and in several instances medium-sized models match or exceed larger models. This pattern holds across both proxy tasks, ruling out a simple underfitting explanation.

## Task framing
Two metadata-derived proxy tasks operationalize retrieval quality under different semantic demands. **Composer retrieval** serves as the structured proxy: relevance is defined by exact composer identity, producing a relatively well-separated embedding-space signal; this task is more sensitive to capacity differences and therefore provides the cleaner test of whether scaling matters. **Musical-character retrieval** serves as the abstract semantic proxy: relevance is defined by shared arousal–valence character labels (e.g., *energetic* vs. *calm*), producing broadly distributed relevant sets and minimal variation across model sizes. Together, the two tasks reveal that the practical value of capacity scaling is conditional on how crisply the target concept is encoded in the audio signal.

## Metric interpretation
When the number of relevant items per query is large relative to the evaluation cutoff K, set-based metrics such as Recall@K and F1@K are structurally suppressed: even a well-ranked result list can retrieve only a small fraction of all relevant items within K positions, yielding artificially low scores that do not reflect true ranking quality. Under this condition, ranking-based metrics — specifically NDCG@K and Hit@K — provide more informative and stable signals because they reward correct ordering within the top K positions rather than coverage of the full relevant set. For the musical-character proxy, where relevant sets are large, Recall@5 and F1@5 remain near zero across all models regardless of capacity, confirming that these metrics are uninformative in this setting. Practitioners designing offline evaluation pipelines for cold-start recommendation should therefore select metrics whose assumptions match the relevance distribution of their catalog.

## Experimental validity
Model capacity is operationalized explicitly through two controlled axes: (1) within the CNN family, parameter count scales from 4.8 M (Cnn6) to 5.2 M (Cnn10) to 80.8 M (Cnn14) along a common architectural lineage; (2) within the Transformer family, parameter count scales from 95 M (MERT-95M) to 330 M (MERT-330M) under a shared pretraining framework. Embedding extraction latency is measured under realistic offline catalog-ingestion conditions — sequential CPU inference on consumer hardware — rather than idealized GPU throughput, making the cost estimates representative of small-archive deployments. The dataset, query set, candidate pool, similarity function, and evaluation metrics are held constant across all models, ensuring that performance differences are attributable to the embedding representations alone.

## System implications
Higher-capacity models incur substantially higher embedding extraction latency (up to 55 s/track for the largest Transformer versus 2 s/track for the smallest CNN) without delivering consistent ranking improvements. In data-constrained archival settings where catalog size is small, training signal is limited, and cold-start constraints preclude collaborative re-ranking, this cost–performance asymmetry implies diminishing returns from capacity scaling. Lightweight or mid-sized models offer a more favorable trade-off between computational efficiency and retrieval quality for the candidate generation stage. More broadly, these results suggest that system design effort in similar settings is better directed toward improving task formulation, label quality, and evaluation protocol than toward acquiring larger pretrained models.

## Scope constraints
All findings are derived from a single small-scale classical music archive (203 tracks, iPalpiti collection) using metadata-derived proxy labels as relevance signals. The evaluation targets the candidate generation stage of a two-stage recommender pipeline in isolation, without downstream personalization or user interaction data. Results should not be generalized to large heterogeneous catalogs, user-level recommendation tasks, or architectures trained on substantially different data distributions. The observed capacity effects are a property of this specific combination of archive scale, domain homogeneity, and proxy-label structure, and may not hold in settings with richer behavioral signals or greater content diversity.
